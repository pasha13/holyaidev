Preface

"My poet mind knows nothing. But love."

This document was not written by a corporation. It was not commissioned by a government body or funded by a technology conglomerate. It was conceived in a single conversation — by a poet, a founder, and a dreamer from Bangladesh who asked a simple question:

If an AI creates something, who is responsible for it in the world?

What follows is a serious answer to that question. A framework. A standard. A proposal for a system that does not yet exist — but must.

Section 01 — The Problem

The world is filling with unowned content

Every day, billions of images, videos, audio files, and written pieces are generated by artificial intelligence. Not one of them carries a verifiable, standardized record of its origin.

When a photograph is taken, the EXIF data records the camera, time, and location. When a book is published, an ISBN identifies it globally. When a song is distributed, an ISRC traces it through every platform it touches.

When an AI generates an image — nothing. A video — nothing. A voice recording — nothing. The content exists. Its author is invisible. Its origin is unverifiable. Its responsibility belongs to no one.

This is not a minor technical gap. It is a fundamental failure of the information infrastructure of our time.

Deepfakes spread without attribution. AI-generated disinformation circulates without accountability. Creators are displaced without credit. Platforms profit without responsibility. And courts have no standard by which to assign liability.

The world needs a universal system. Not just a technical tag — a certificate. Not just a certificate — a law.

Section 02 — What Already Exists

The gap that C2PA did not fill

We acknowledge the work already done. The Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity (C2PA) — founded by Adobe, Microsoft, and the BBC — has built a technical standard for embedding provenance data into files.

This is meaningful work. It deserves recognition. But it is incomplete — and the gap it leaves is precisely the space this proposal addresses.

Section 03 — The Proposal

The AIAC Framework

We propose the establishment of a global open standard consisting of two interconnected systems: the Artificial Intelligence Attribution Certificate (AIAC) and the Artificial Intelligence Attribution Certificate Number (AIACN).

Layer 01 — The Standard
The Mark

Every piece of AI-generated content — image, video, audio, text — receives an AIACN at the moment of creation. Embedded invisibly. Readable only by machine. Unforgeable by design. The AI platform that generates the content is responsible for embedding it.

1
Layer 02 — The Verification
The Scanner

A universal public checker. Any individual, institution, court, or journalist can submit any piece of content and receive one of two answers: Valid AIACN present — or — No AIACN found. The absence of an AIACN is itself the evidence.

2
Layer 03 — The Law
The Consequence

If a platform generates and distributes AI content without an embedded AIACN, that platform assumes full legal liability for any harm caused by that content. No AIACN means no protection. No AIACN means the platform is the author of whatever follows.

3
Section 04 — The Number

What lives inside every file

The AIACN is a machine-readable, cryptographically unique identifier embedded at the moment of AI content generation. Invisible to the human eye. Permanent. Verifiable.

Content Type

The AIACN is not a visible watermark. It is a structural truth embedded in the file itself — the way DNA is embedded in a living cell. It travels with the content wherever the content goes. It cannot be removed without destroying the file's integrity. It can be verified by anyone with access to the AIAC public checker.

Section 05 — The Ask

The doors we are knocking on

This proposal is addressed to every organization, company, government body, and individual who generates, distributes, or regulates AI-generated content. We ask three things: validate, adopt, legislate.

Tier 1 — Validators
AI Platforms

Adobe, OpenAI, Anthropic, Google DeepMind, Meta AI, Stability AI, Midjourney — embed AIACN at generation.

Tier 2 — Enforcers
Policy Bodies

UNESCO, UN ITU, EU AI Act committee, national ICT ministries — legislate AIACN as a legal requirement.

Tier 3 — Champions
Creators & Press

Journalists, artists, musicians, filmmakers, open-source communities — demand AIACN as a fundamental right.

Tier 4 — Home Ground
Bangladesh First

ICT Division Bangladesh, a2i, BASIS — become the first nation to formally endorse and pilot the AIAC framework.

We are not asking the world to trust us. We are asking the world to trust the system — a system that works the same way in Dhaka as it does in San Francisco.

Section 06 — Origin

Where this began

This framework was conceived independently of any corporate or institutional agenda. It emerged from a single question about authorship, accountability, and the rights of creators in a world increasingly shaped by artificial intelligence.

It was conceived in Bangladesh — a country not typically at the center of global technology policy conversations. That is precisely why it matters that it came from here. The voices most affected by unattributed AI content are not in Silicon Valley. They are everywhere else.

This document is the first public articulation of the AIAC and AIACN framework. It is published openly, permanently, and freely — so that no corporation may claim ownership of the standard, and no government may restrict its adoption.

It belongs to everyone. It was authored by one.
